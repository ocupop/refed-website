Each year, 52.4 million tons of food are thrown into landfills annually, and another 10.1 million tons is estimated to be discarded or left unharvested on farms and in packinghouses.

This mountain of wasted calories, resources, and energy grows up to two times larger when you add in other food fit for human consumption yet still ends up being composted, converted into animal feed, or discarded in other ways.

Food waste has recently emerged as an urgent global issue.

In 2012, the European Parliament passed a resolution to halve food waste in the European Union by 2025. In 2015, the U.S. government declared a similar national 50% food waste reduction goal by 2030. And as part of its Sustainable Development Goals, the United Nations agreed on the need to halve per capita food waste in the consumer and retail sectors and reduce food losses along production and supply chains by 2030.

What is Food Waste?

Various organizations define food loss and waste in different ways. ReFED has adopted the U.S. Department of Agriculture definition of “food waste,” which refers to wasted food that happens anywhere along the supply chain and includes all types of food loss and waste as defined by other leading institutions. Food waste is a subset of organic waste, which includes anything biodegradable that comes from plants or animals, such as yard trimmings and manure. Food waste includes unavoidable scraps, such as bones and rinds that retain beneficial value for reuse. It does not include waste from crop varieties specifically grown for fuel, animal feed, or other commercial uses.

Where Does Food Waste Occur?

Addressable food waste can be found throughout the supply chain, which can be simplified into four segments: farms, food manufacturers, consumer-facing businesses (including distributors, retail grocers, restaurants, foodservice providers, and institutions), and homes (including all types of dwellings).

Policy Overview

Public policy at the federal, state, and municipal levels can offer opportunities to accelerate large-scale food waste reduction. The Food Waste Policy Finder – developed by ReFED and the Harvard Law School Food Law and Policy Clinic to provide an overview of current federal and state policies related to food waste – depicts the existing policy landscape, while highlighting best-practice legislation in order to inform the continued development and implementation of sound food waste policy.
