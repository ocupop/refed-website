A cost effective approach to food waste

ReFED set out to understand the most cost-effective ways to reduce food waste and the resources needed to implement those solutions at scale. A wide list of solutions was gathered from stakeholders, and narrowed to a short list of 27 solutions that met criteria around data availability, cost-effectiveness, feasibility, and scalability.

Read more about ReFED's methodology  >
ReFED developed a core economic model to perform three analyses on the solutions:
  1. A total net societal “Economic Value” based on their cost-effectiveness per ton of waste
  2. The business profit potential that the private sector can earn by investing in each solution
  3. The non-financial impacts of each solution — meals saved, greenhouse gases reduced, jobs created, water conserved

Solutions across the supply chain

Each segment of the food supply chain has role to play in reducing our country’s food waste, but restaurants and retail businesses have the opportunity to scale up a majority of the analyzed solutions. (Some solutions can be implemented by more than one supply chain category.)

Restaurant and retail businesses impact waste both upstream (farmers and manufacturers) and downstream (consumers), so new corporate policies from a few large businesses can have a powerful trickle-down effect up and down the entire value chain.

Profiting From Food Waste

Rather than burdening the bottom line, 11 of the solutions represent $2.3 billion in business profit potential. Nine are geared toward prevention and two toward recycling. They all create revenue and cost savings opportunities that can be captured by the businesses that implement them.

Restaurants and foodservice facilities have the largest profit opportunity.
