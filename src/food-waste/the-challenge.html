Various organizations define food waste in different ways. ReFED has conducted our analysis using surplus food – defined as all food that goes unsold or unused by a business or that goes uneaten at home – including food and inedible parts (e.g., peels, pits, bones) that are donated, fed to animals, repurposed to produce other products, composted, or anaerobically digested. Because this includes donated food, we have subtracted these donations to generate our estimates of uneaten food. We use the term food waste as a subset of uneaten food to describe food that goes unused and ends up being landfilled, incinerated, disposed down the sewer, dumped, or spread onto land. The U.S. Government has set a 50% landfill diversion goal, that aims to cut the amount of food that goes to landfills and incineration in half. International goals, such as United Nations Sustainable Development Goal 12.3 or the European Union’s goals, aim to cut in half the amount of food going to eight destinations, including composting and anaerobic digestion, in addition to landfill. Businesses may be reluctant to implement a new technology or process if another part of the supply chain receives the benefit. They are also not incentivized to reduce the waste of their customers, as this could ultimately reduce sales.

- title: Lack Of Social License description_html: >-

Consumer expectations for product variety, convenience, low prices, and cosmetic perfection constrain businesses from streamlining their selection, offering cosmetically imperfect food, and reducing portion sizes.

- title: Information Gaps description_html: >-

There is uncertainty about where food waste occurs, how much is being wasted, and its associated value. As such, the costs of food waste are often invisible, and it’s difficult to manage when it’s not being measured. [Visit ReFED's Insights Engine for up-to-date facts about food waste.]

- title: Organizational Silos description_html: >-

Implementing food waste reduction solutions requires collaboration between different departments within participating businesses, including buyers, merchants, store managers, chefs, waste managers, and financial analysts. Employees in different parts of an organization may not be aware of the fully loaded cost implications of waste.

- title: Liability Concerns description_html: >-

The federal Good Samaritan Food Donation Act protects donors and recipients from civil or criminal liability short of gross negligence and misconduct. Still, many are not aware of this protection, and even those who are may have concerns over public perception that could result from a food safety issue around donation. Food recovery organizations have robust systems to ensure safety, but many businesses are still skeptical.

- title: Fragmented Regulation description_html: >-

Health regulations vary by city and state, arising from “home rule” authority in some localities and differing interpretations of the FDA Food Code, which only loosely defines basic requirements for food safety. This hampers national and global companies from developing uniform food donation policies across their organizations.

- title: 'Handling, Transportation, And Storage' description_html: >-

Many consumer-facing businesses lack sufficient facilities to store food for donation. Food banks, pantries, soup kitchens, and other community organizations may similarly not have sufficient infrastructure or labor capacity to accept large donation volumes, especially of perishable foods requiring refrigeration or freezing. [Check out our Solution Provider Directory to find organizations that can help support your food waste reduction initiatives.]

- title: Financial Viability description_html: >-

Donating food can involve additional handling costs beyond regular disposal. For example, state and federal tax benefits for recovered food rarely cover the farm labor costs involved. Without a way to recoup these costs, businesses are more likely to default to the path of least resistance: disposal.

- title: Cost Of Disposal description_html: >-

Landfill disposal rates (i.e., tipping fees) have remained exceptionally low in the U.S., relative to many other developed countries. Since tipping fees are typically the largest revenue streams for recycling processing facilities, this has hurt the business case to expand organics recycling infrastructure.

- title: Material Supply Assurance description_html: >-

A long-term guarantee of material is necessary for recycling facilities to access project finance and maintain long-term profitability. However, most cities and businesses are reluctant to sign long-term waste supply contracts due to long-term price uncertainty.

- title: High Transportation And Logistics Costs description_html: >-

Once waste is collected and deposited to a transfer station, the distance that it must travel to a recycling facility versus a landfill becomes the key driver for system economics. If hauling costs to a recycling facility are high, haulers may not bring enough food scrap volumes to keep the facility at the high capacity rate needed to be profitable.

- title: Access To Financing description_html: >-

Food waste reduction solutions have varying return profiles based on their level of maturity, capital required, nature of the solution (for-profit, non-profit), and more. This can result in a lack – or perceived lack – of direct return on investment, competing internal initiatives (from a management/personnel bandwidth and budgeting perspective) at the adopter level, and poor matching of capital type to the appropriate project. A perceived risk of adopting new solutions (e.g., new technology, operational disruptions, etc.) leads to higher return hurdles required to adopt the project. Additionally, capital-intensive projects require high upfront costs and are the most sensitive to variations in financing rates, which can be a differentiator for success. [Learn about the financing needed to implement the food waste reduction solutions in our Insights Engine here.]

- title: Packaging And Contamination description_html: >-

The hassle of removing food from its packaging significantly reduces food recycling rates among businesses and consumers. Common contaminants include plastic, foam, or disposable food packaging that appears compostable but is not. The problem continues at the treatment facility. Compost or anaerobic digestion facilities that receive highly contaminated feedstock must spend more costs on pre- and post-processing, which may hurt profitability.

- title: Permitting And Siting description_html: >-

Compost and anaerobic digestion facilities have trouble rallying local support, because the benefits are often hidden to communities. For densely populated municipalities, a limited number of sites are feasible for centralized composting. In addition, communities often object to compost facilities from “NIMBY” concerns related to perceived odors, pests, or increased truck traffic, even though these impacts can vary greatly based on the management of the compost facility.

- title: End-Market Development description_html: >-

Recycling facilities must maintain high prices for their end products (compost, digestate, energy) to maintain financial viability over time. This is impacted by customer demand. Energy demand is massive and linked into regional markets. However, for anaerobic digestion projects, the sharp ups and downs of natural gas market prices make it challenging to finance projects that require stable, long-term cash flows. Compost markets are smaller and constrained by transportation costs. Therefore, market demand for compost must keep pace with the millions of new tons of compost generated, or else a market imbalance will negatively impact compost prices and system economics.

Each year, 92.8 million tons of food in the U.S. – more than 567 pounds for each person – are never eaten. In fact, food is the number one product entering landfills today. 

The impacts on our climate and environmental resources are enormous, and it’s a huge waste of nutrition as well – much of that food could have gone to people struggling with food insecurity, a number that has grown due to the ongoing economic downturn caused by the coronavirus pandemic. Around the world, wasted food is considered to be an urgent issue – the United Nations, U.S. Government, European Parliament, global business coalitions such as the Consumer Goods Forum, and more have all set goals to cut food loss and waste in half by 2025 or 2030.

Climate and Natural Resources

When food goes uneaten, the resources used to produce it go to waste as well. If all of our country’s uneaten food was grown in one place, this “mega-farm” would cover roughly 80 million acres, over three-quarters of the entire state of California – all for food that is never eaten.

Food Insecurity

One in six Americans – many of them children – are food insecure, meaning they lack reliable access to sufficient, affordable, nutritious, and culturally appropriate food. And because of the impact of COVID-19, the number of people struggling with food insecurity grew in 2020 to more than 50 million, according to Feeding America. A huge portion of surplus food is perfectly edible and could go to help those in need.

Economy

Each year, the country lets food worth nearly $400 billion a year go uneaten. The financial cost of this is greatest for consumers, since they pay retail prices for food while consumer-facing and other businesses pay lower wholesale rates. But the food industry is responsible for approximately $240 billion each year, with foodservice businesses shouldering the greatest burden.

Where does food waste occur?

Food waste is often considered to be a singular problem, but it’s an entirely different situation when hundreds of tons of broccoli go unharvested on a farm compared to a half-full platter of uneaten potatoes being scraped into the trash at home. Waste occurs at each stage of the supply chain, with the majority happening at consumer-facing businesses – like grocery stores, restaurants, and other businesses that sell and serve food – and in homes. But because food waste is also systemic in nature, it’s important to recognize that what happens at one stage is often influenced by something that happens at another stage, either upstream or downstream. For example, the farm referenced above may not harvest all of its broccoli, because it doesn’t meet appearance specifications set by the retailers that purchase its produce.

85% of food waste occurring downstream at consumer-facing businesses and homes

Which foods get wasted

Nearly 80% of surplus food is perishable food, which includes fruits and vegetables, meats, prepared fresh deli items, seafood, milk and dairy, and some grain products such as bread and bakery items. Perishables often get discarded, because they can be inexpensive and quickly go bad. In contrast, non-perishable foods — pastas, canned goods, and highly processed, shelf-stable products — are generally wasted less, because they don’t spoil as easily. Pound per pound, fruits and vegetables are among the least expensive and fastest-spoiling foods; not surprisingly, they constitute more than 35% of total food waste. Conversely, seafood and meats are the most expensive food types and the two least wasted.

What causes food waste?

The reasons for food waste are numerous and complex across the food supply chain. And with the advent of COVID-19, the challenges of keeping food from going to waste have only grown. Here are some insights into why it occurs:

Farms

Food loss starts at the production level. Low market prices and high harvest costs often make it uneconomical for farmers to gather all that they produce. Strict cosmetic standards result in insufficient demand for imperfect-looking produce. Labor shortages cause further challenges. Despite gleaning and farm-to-food-bank efforts to recover unharvested food, the vast majority is left in the fields and tilled under. With foodservice closings during COVID, farms that typically sold to those markets found themselves with huge surpluses that ultimately went to waste.

Learn More

Manufacturers

The vast majority of surplus in food processing is byproducts – the peels, stems, bones, and other parts not used in the main product. Many of these trimmings, such as potato peels from french fry lines, are still edible. The wide variety of products sold causes inefficiencies as well – each time a production line is changed, it must be emptied and cleaned. During the COVID-19 pandemic, some food manufacturers saw a tripling in demand, but others struggled to adjust their output to meet the needs of new customers with different product requirements – for example, manufacturers who had always produced milk in large containers for industrial markets were unable to quickly begin offering their products in smaller, consumer-friendly sizes.

Learn More

Retailers

Customer demand for a variety and quality of food being readily available strains inventory management and food purchasing, and it can be difficult to change stocking practices or product sizes – especially when those practices are tied to a retailer's brand identity. High customer standards for freshness can lead businesses to dispose of safe, edible food based on a perception that it's past its prime. When the COVID-19 pandemic began, retailers faced unexpected upticks in demand, as consumers rushed to stock up and make bulk purchases to limit their trips to the store; many retailers found it difficult to accurately order correct amounts of stock, leading to shortages and waste.

Learn More

Homes

Poor food management leads to spoilage and ultimately drives food waste in homes. Many consumer purchases are unplanned, which can lead to over-purchasing, and many families are tempted into bulk purchases of food they will never consume to get a good deal on per-unit costs. Many consumers also lack the knowledge of how to repurpose ingredients and store food properly. (It’s important to note that some of this is changing due to the coronavirus pandemic, which has driven consumers to prepare more meals at home, thereby helping to improve their food management and kitchen skills.) Misunderstanding of date labels often leads consumers to throw away food before it’s spoiled, causing an estimated eight percent of at-home food waste. Many consumers also have limited access to municipal food waste recycling programs and perceive barriers to composting at home.

Learn More

Foodservice

Similar to retailers, foodservice customers expect a variety and quality of food to be available – and in the case of fast-food restaurants and other businesses with similar formats, customers expect it as soon as they place their order. Difficulty in forecasting, bulk purchasing, and improper storage can all lead to waste, along with preparation techniques that can leave usable food behind. Foodservice businesses have been among the hardest hit by the COVID-19 pandemic, as mandated lockdowns and ongoing customer apprehensions over safety have led to huge decreases in business. Sudden closures left many restaurants with excess food, which they struggled to repurpose through different selling methods (e.g., creating mini-markets to sell unused ingredients) or donations.
